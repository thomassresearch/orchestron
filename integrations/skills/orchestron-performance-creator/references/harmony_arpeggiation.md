# Harmony, Voicing, and Arpeggiation

Use this reference when generating melodic sequencers, chord pads, bass relationships, arpeggiators, or harmonic score specs.

## Harmony serves motion

In ambient, techno, trance, Goa, and psytrance, harmony is usually subordinate to rhythm, texture, and energy architecture. Frequent chord changes are rarely the main event.

Style tendencies:

- Ambient: stable modal or tonal centers, slow color shifts, drones, and spacious phrases.
- Techno: pedal-based ideas, short riffs, stabs, and rhythmic treatment of pitch.
- Trance: harmony supports the emotional wave of breakdown, buildup, and arrival.
- Goa/psy: modal or scalar riff families sit above a bass-and-drum engine.

Use chord changes when they change state or energy. Otherwise, let register, density, rhythm, filters, and layering do the work.

## Treat chord labels as color states

Orchestron chord labels are useful as compact color controls. Use simple states for exposed grooves and richer states when texture has more space:

- Stable/simple: `none`, `maj`, `min`, `sus2`, `sus4`.
- Warm or emotional: `min7`, `maj7`, `add9` if available in the target surface.
- Tension/dominant: `dom7`, `dim`, `aug`, `m7b5`, `dim7`, `minmaj7`.

When the GUI or CLI does not support a desired color label, choose the closest supported label from `references/chord_syntax.md` and place extra color in another melodic layer or arpeggiator.

## Voice leading over chord density

Prefer smooth connections between adjacent harmonic states:

- Retain common tones between chords.
- Use inversions to avoid blocky root motion.
- Keep bass function simple when upper harmony is colorful.
- Use open voicings for chord stabs when possible.
- Use closed voicings when an arpeggiator should make a compact contour.

Dense chords can reduce groove impact when the rhythm is exposed. Reserve richer sonorities for ambient states, intros, breakdowns, or late arrivals where color is meant to dominate.

## Bass relationships

For groove-first material, the bass should make the metric and modal center clear:

- Techno: pedal notes, short cells, and controlled offbeat pressure.
- Trance: bass supports the arrival and should feel clear when kick returns.
- Goa: syncopated or additive bass can contrast with a straighter drum frame.
- Psy: repetitive drone or rolling bass may be the main identity of the track.
- Ambient: bass can be a sustained root, fifth, or slow moving foundation.

Do not over-write the bass if another layer carries the syncopation. A bassline can be structurally strong because it is consistent.

## Arpeggiators as formal devices

Treat arpeggiators as rhythmic surfaces that expose a harmonic state over time:

- Techno: secondary pulse layer that energizes space between stabs.
- Trance: density builder that points toward an arrival while preserving chord identity.
- Goa/psy: core syntax through 16th-note triad or scalar figures, octave alternation, modal motion, and small-interval gestures.
- Ambient: slow shimmer, register drift, or sparse repeated gesture.

Useful arp variants:

- Base: same chord state, steady subdivision.
- Heightened: increased rate, higher register, or wider range.
- Tension: repeated leading tone, suspended chord color, filter rise, or delayed resolution.
- Reduced: sparse notes, lower velocity, or missing downbeat.

Connect arp variants to pad-loop functions so arpeggiation helps the arrangement move rather than acting as decoration.
