# Genre Blueprints

Use this reference when the user asks for a style-specific Orchestron performance or when a draft needs stronger genre identity.

## Ambient

Write ambient as a sequence of states rather than pop-style sections. Ambient can be beatless or lightly pulsed; its form often suggests open-ended duration.

Useful plan:

1. Environment established.
2. First tonal cue.
3. Slight brightening, thickening, or register shift.
4. Optional crest.
5. Gradual dissipation.

Writing priorities:

- Stable modal or tonal center.
- Drones, slow pads, and sparse gestures.
- Long controller curves for brightness, width, or density.
- Few cadential closures.
- Slow transformation instead of constant new material.

Orchestron mapping:

- Use melodic pads for long harmonic states.
- Use controller pads for slow sweeps.
- Use pad loops with long pauses or repeated states.
- Keep drummer tracks sparse or omit them.

## Techno

Write techno from the groove outward. Start with a stable 4/4 engine, a strong 16th-note pulse ecology, and a limited harmonic field. Form comes from process.

Useful plan:

1. Sparse intro.
2. First core plateau.
3. Progressive accretion.
4. Reduced plateau or breakdown.
5. Denser second core.
6. Sparse outro.

Writing priorities:

- Four-on-the-floor anchor.
- Repetitive machine-like layers.
- Powers-of-two phrase grouping.
- Subtle variation in hats, bass, filter, register, or off-grid counter-layer.
- Limited chord motion.

Orchestron mapping:

- Use drummer `four_on_floor` or a direct groove equivalent.
- Use short melodic pads for stabs, bass cells, or pedal patterns.
- Use odd-length controller or counter-layer loops against a stable main loop.
- Use supergroups for intro, plateau, reduction, second plateau, and outro.

## Trance

Write trance as controlled waves of withholding and return. The breakdown, buildup, and arrival must contrast clearly.

Useful plan:

1. Intro groove.
2. First buildup.
3. Breakdown with thinned texture.
4. Snare, arp, sweep, or density buildup.
5. Arrival with kick and bass return.
6. Second wave or outro.

Writing priorities:

- Clear harmonic or melodic identity.
- Breakdown that genuinely removes rhythmic weight.
- Buildup through increasing density, dynamics, register, filter brightness, snare activity, or arp rate.
- Arrival defined by the return of established groove elements.

Orchestron mapping:

- Use reduced pads for breakdown states.
- Use controller pads for long filter and brightness sweeps.
- Use arp or melodic heightened variants for buildup.
- Use pad-loop supergroups for wave cycles.

## Goa Trance

Write classic Goa as a signature riff ecology with long accumulative/subtractive arcs and a late peak.

Useful plan:

1. Pulse and bass frame.
2. First modal riff.
3. Counter-layer and subdivision thickening.
4. Reduced or simplified passage.
5. Renewed climb with stronger riff identity.
6. Late climax and release.

Writing priorities:

- Modal color, often Phrygian, Phrygian-major, harmonic-minor, or related scalar collections.
- Small-second gestures, octave alternation, and scalar cells.
- Groupings of three, 3+3+2 accents, and gaps inside 16th-note chains.
- Syncopated or additive bass against a straighter drum frame.
- Combined texture that keeps many 16th-note subdivisions alive.

Orchestron mapping:

- Use melodic pads for related riff variants rather than unrelated melodies.
- Use pad loops to climb through base, heightened, tension, and climax forms.
- Use controller curves to create long rises.
- Use drummer and bass tracks as the stable frame while lead/counter layers create rhythmic friction.

## Psytrance

Write psytrance from a groove-and-bass ecology, then mutate lead, texture, and controller motion around it.

Useful plan:

1. Kick/bass motor.
2. Dark or modal texture state.
3. Mutating lead fragments.
4. Tension rise through density and filter motion.
5. Drop or renewed motor.
6. Simplification or outro.

Writing priorities:

- Repetitive bass-centered motor.
- Drone or darker pitch material when appropriate.
- Lead mutation instead of constant melodic replacement.
- Tight rhythmic contrast against a stable grid.
- Long-form accumulation and release.

Orchestron mapping:

- Keep the bass pattern consistent enough to define the track.
- Use melodic pads for lead mutations and tension variants.
- Use controller pads heavily for timbral movement.
- Use supergroups for extended rises and renewed climbs.
